Abilities

All characters have acccess to the same set of basic Abilities, which are influenced by the most appropriate Attribute:

Endurance

Endurance is a character's cardio. In essence, how long can an effort be sustained before you run out of steam. While there is no specific combat-Action based on Endurance, your GM can ask you for an Endurance roll if you perform repeated Sprinting attempts or other strenuous tasks.

Endurance can also be used to help calculate travel times for long distance trips, etc.

Resistance

Resistance is one of the less-used abilities. Generally, it applies as a save against hostile attempts to affect your character, such as Grappling attempts or attacks that attempt to stun your character.

Some GMs also like to use the Resistance ability to reduce the potency of certain effects, such as poisons, but this is up to the GM and should be discussed during your group's Session Zero.

Athletics

Athletics is a measure of the direct physical force a character's body can muster on short notice. Trying to push a heavy object, lift a rock, or compete in an arm-wrestling match are all typical examples that would warrant an Athletics roll.

In combat, the most common Athletics check you will see is the Sprint Action:

A Sprint Action is an attempt to gain additional Movement so that you can move across the battlefield more quickly, or run away from an overpowering enemy. If you spend a combat-Action to try to sprint:

  • less than 0 => no bonus Movement (the Action is still spent, though!)
  • 0 to 9 => +1 m of Movement
  • 10 to 19 => +2 m of Movement
  • 20 to 29 => +3 m of Movement
  • etc.

Grappling

Grappling is an attempt to hold an enemy down, preventing them from moving and using Actions freely. This can be done to protect your allies, prevent an enemy from running away, or simply to stop a spellcaster from casting. Grappling is almost exclusively a combat action, which means if you attempt it in a non-combat encounter, it will usually turn into a combat encounter.

First, the initiating grappler rolls their atk roll, which is a Grappling ability roll with Unarmed Proficiency. The resulting number is then compared to the target's DV to see if the target simply dodges the attack altogether. Finally, if the target does not dodge, the defender also rolls a Grappling roll to determine who gains control in the struggle. The initial Grappling attempt does not deal any damage.

If the attacker gains control, the Grapple is maintained, and the attacker continues their turn. If the defender gains control, they may choose to immediately let go of the Grapple, or maintain it as is. Regardless of who gains control, both contestants gain the Grappled effect as long as the Grapple is maintained.

While grappled, the contestants can only perform the following Actions (rolling with disadvantage unless they are in control of the contest):

  • Gain Control: The character performing this Action simply re-rolls their Grappling attempt. The DC for this is calculated according to the normal DC formula, based on the opponent's most recent Grappling roll. This Action can also be used to improve already existing control of a Grappling contest; however, it does not deal any damage.
  • Shove Action: The initiator rolls Athletics, while the defender rolls Resistance. The winner may then dictate up to 1 m of movement for each 5 points of lead in the contest. For example, if the contest results are 5 vs 18, the character that rolled the 18 can move both characters by up to 2 m each ((18 - 5) / 5 = 2). Such Movment is only possible if the winner actually has the necessary Movement available, but the looser's Movement is ignored within this context.

Intimidation

An Intimidation Action is an attempt to frighten the enemy, which is represented by the Feared effect when in combat. It functions similar to the Grappling contest, except that the initiator rolls Intimidation, while the target rolls Leadership as a measure of their level of discipline.

Intimidation is usually specific to the one target, but can in some cases also apply to a group of targets. In such situations, the GM will ususally increase the DC based on how many targets are around. In other words, the more allies a target has around them, the higher their chance of resisting an Intimidation attempt.

Acrobatics

Acrobatics represents a character's general agility.

In many instances, a character may need to perform a Jump Action, for example to cross a chasm, to avoid triggering a pressure plate trap, etc.:

  • less than 0 => the jump critically fails, which may mean the PC falls into the chasm, triggers the trap, etc. without the opportunity to try to save the situation
  • 0 to 4 => the jump fails, but the PC may roll a Reflex save to stop themselves. The DC for the save is 20 - the existing Acrobatics roll
  • 5 to 9 => the jump clears up to 1 m
  • 10 to 14 => the jump clears up to 2 m
  • 15 to 19 => the jump clears up to 3 m
  • 20 to 24 => the jump clears up to 4 m
  • 25 to 29 => the jump clears up to 5 m
  • etc.

Note that the Jump distance does count towards your Movement when you are in combat. In other words, if your PC has only 3 tiles of Movement left, they can only jump up to 3 tiles, even if they roll a wicked Acrobatics check (let's say a 42 or something like that).

Reflex

Reflex is primarily a save against enemy attempts to place a character under an effect. It can also be used as a save when the character experiences sudden unexpected (and usually very negative) situations.

For example, if your character has to climb a rope to cross a deep chasm, rolling very low on an Acrobatics check could result in your character falling to their death. Rather than having such high stakes rely upon a single roll, the GM may allow the falling character a Reflex save to try to catch the rope again.

In combat, retreat may sometimes be the most viable strategy. A Disengage Action therefore represents an attempt to leave an enemy's melee range, but as you will see in the Attack of Opportunity section, this may give the enemy a potential opportunity to attack even though it is not their turn. The disengage is the fleeing character's attempt to avoid this attack, by spending an Action of their own to contest the enemy's atk with a Reflex ability roll.

Stealth

Sneaking represents an attempt to prevent an enemy from knowing where you are or what you are doing. While, mathematically, this is represented by a simple ability check, the actual chance of success is much more complicated:

At minimum, you will likely have to break line-of-sight, and reduce the noises you make. You'll have to carefully examine the battlemap and decide where you want to position yourself, and then move as little as possible.

Unfortunately, your GM will most likely not tell you if your attempt at stealth is successful or not until the consequences are already self-evident...

In combat, if you manage to stay hidden, the first attack you make from a successful stealth attempt counts as a Sneak Attack, which means the dmg roll for the atk will be multiplied by 2.

Successful Sneak Attack = dmg *2

Pickpocket Action (Thievery)

As a combat action, pickpocketing represents an attempt to manipulate your enemy's inventory. This can be straightforward as grabbing a key from their belt, or as subtle as switching one of their healing potions for a poison flask from your own inventory. Obviously, if the enemy has already spotted you, the DC for the attempt will be higher, but distractions in the form of Grappling or Performance rolls can sometimes help you achieve success.

For obvious reasons, this action can only be performed if you are in melee range of your target.

Knowledge Check (History)

A History check is essential when special enemy types appear on the battlefield. Certain enemies will either be immune to certain damage types, or may even be healed or strengthened by them instead. Think of a fire elemental, for example. Not only does it not care about you waving a torch in its face, but stoking its flames would actually make it stronger.

The History check is therefore there to try to find out what the enemy may be vulnerable to, or resistant against.

Targeting Action (Investigation)

Taking a second to pick your target's weak spot is useful, especially if your enemy has not seen you yet. It essentially sacrifices one action in order to make the next attack stronger:

  • less than 0 => no bonus
  • 0 to 4 => +1 atk and +2 dmg
  • 5 to 9 => +2 atk and +4 dmg
  • 10 to 14 => +3 atk and +6 dmg
  • 15 to 19 => +4 atk and +8 dmg
  • 20 to 24 => +5 atk and +10 dmg
  • 25 to 29 => +6 atk and +12 dmg

Intuition Check

If you're stuck and not really sure what to do, you can also use an Intuition check to essentaily ask your GM for some hints. A Knowledge check can only give you information that your PC would reasonably know based on their backstory and previous encounters, but a good Intuition check might allow you to infer brand new conclusions.

Perception Check

This is a quick physical check of your surroundings. If you suspect that there might be enemies in hiding, waiting to ambush you, or if you think an area might be trapped, it may be a good idea to spend an Action just making sure you have a good lay of the land before charging in.

As for all abilities, if you do not make a specific roll, your Passive Perception is 0 + your modifiers. In other words, even if you get the lowest possible roll on your ability check, you will get more information than simply waiting for the GM to tell you what's happening. Some GMs will wait for you to ask them for a Perception check, rather than potentially giving you a hint that there might be something hidden by asking you for it.

Inspiration Action

If you have a very critical roll coming up, you can spnd an Action to psych yourself up for it. This is very similar to the Targeting Action for atk rolls, but for ability checks. Note that the bonuses are reduced:

  • less than 0 => no bonus
  • 0 to 9 => +1 next ability roll
  • 10 to 19 => +2 next ability roll
  • 20 to 29 => +3 next ability roll

You will also roll Inspiration every time you spend a Party Point on yourself, although in that case it does not cost you an Action.

Help Others Action (Leadership)

In the same spirit as the Inspiration Action, you can also spend an Action to help one of your allies. These bonuses are higher, though, especially if you roll well:

  • less than 0 => no bonus
  • 0 to 4 => +1
  • 5 to 9 => +2
  • 10 to 14 => +3
  • 15 to 19 => +4
  • 20 to 24 => +5
  • 25 to 29 => +6

When you spend Party Points on your allies instead of yourself, you will also be able to use the Leadership roll instead of the Inspiration roll.

Distraction Action (Performance)

This is essentially the opposite of a Sneak Action, and can be used to help take the attention away from your allies so that they can perform a critical task more safely, or to lure enemies into a trap. The GM will determine the DC of the roll based on the specific context, including your role-play for the distraction.
