Abilities

All characters have acccess to the same set of basic Abilities, which are influenced by the most appropriate Attribute:

Endurance

Endurance is a character's cardio. In essence, how long can an effort be sustained before you run out of steam. While there is no specific combat-Action based on Endurance, your GM can ask you for an Endurance roll if you perform repeated Sprinting attempts or other strenuous tasks.

Endurance can also be used to help calculate travel times for long distance trips, etc.

Resistance

Resistance is one of the less-used abilities. Generally, it applies as a save against hostile attempts to affect your character, such as Grappling attempts or attacks that attempt to stun your character.

Some GMs also like to use the Resistance ability to reduce the potency of certain effects, such as poisons, but this is up to the GM and should be discussed during your group's Session Zero.

Athletics

Athletics is a measure of the direct physical force a character's body can muster on short notice. Trying to push a heavy object, lift a rock, or compete in an arm-wrestling match are all typical examples that would warrant an Athletics roll.

In combat, the most common Athletics check you will see is the Sprint Action:

A Sprint Action is an attempt to gain additional Movement so that you can move across the battlefield more quickly, or run away from an overpowering enemy. If you spend a combat-Action to try to sprint:

  • less than 0 => no bonus Movement (the Action is still spent, though!)
  • 0 to 9 => +1 m of Movement
  • 10 to 19 => +2 m of Movement
  • 20 to 29 => +3 m of Movement
  • etc.

Grappling

Grappling is an attempt to hold an enemy down, preventing them from moving and using Actions freely. This can be done to protect your allies, prevent an enemy from running away, or simply to stop a spellcaster from casting. Grappling is almost exclusively a combat action, which means if you attempt it in a non-combat encounter, it will usually turn into a combat encounter.

First, the initiating grappler rolls their atk roll, which is a Grappling ability roll with Unarmed Proficiency. The resulting number is then compared to the target's DV to see if the target simply dodges the attack altogether. Finally, if the target does not dodge, the defender also rolls a Grappling roll to determine who gains control in the struggle. The initial Grappling attempt does not deal any damage.

If the attacker gains control, the Grapple is maintained, and the attacker continues their turn. If the defender gains control, they may choose to immediately let go of the Grapple, or maintain it as is. Regardless of who gains control, both contestants gain the Grappled effect as long as the Grapple is maintained.

While grappled, the contestants can only perform the following Actions (rolling with disadvantage unless they are in control of the contest):

  • Gain Control: The character performing this Action simply re-rolls their Grappling attempt. The DC for this is calculated according to the normal DC formula, based on the opponent's most recent Grappling roll. This Action can also be used to improve already existing control of a Grappling contest; however, it does not deal any damage.
  • Shove Action: The initiator rolls Athletics, while the defender rolls Resistance. The winner may then dictate up to 1 m of movement for each 5 points of lead in the contest. For example, if the contest results are 5 vs 18, the character that rolled the 18 can move both characters by up to 2 m each ((18 - 5) / 5 = 2). Such Movment is only possible if the winner actually has the necessary Movement available, but the looser's Movement is ignored within this context.
  • Unarmed Attack
  • Intimidation Action
  • Pickpocket Action
  • Knowledge Check

Intimidation

An Intimidation Action is an attempt to frighten the enemy, which is represented by the Feared effect when in combat. It functions similar to the Grappling contest, except that the initiator rolls Intimidation, while the target rolls Leadership as a measure of their level of discipline.

Intimidation is usually specific to the one target, but can in some cases also apply to a group of targets. In such situations, the GM will ususally increase the DC based on how many targets are around. In other words, the more allies a target has around them, the higher their chance of resisting an Intimidation attempt.

Acrobatics

Acrobatics represents a character's general agility.

In many instances, a character may need to perform a Jump Action, for example to cross a chasm, to avoid triggering a pressure plate trap, etc.:

  • less than 0 => the jump critically fails, which may mean the PC falls into the chasm, triggers the trap, etc. without the opportunity to try to save the situation
  • 0 to 4 => the jump fails, but the PC may roll a Reflex save to stop themselves. The DC for the save is 20 - the existing Acrobatics roll
  • 5 to 9 => the jump clears up to 1 m
  • 10 to 14 => the jump clears up to 2 m
  • 15 to 19 => the jump clears up to 3 m
  • 20 to 24 => the jump clears up to 4 m
  • 25 to 29 => the jump clears up to 5 m
  • etc.

Note that the Jump distance does count towards your Movement when you are in combat. In other words, if your PC has only 3 tiles of Movement left, they can only jump up to 3 tiles, even if they roll a wicked Acrobatics check (let's say a 42 or something like that).

Reflex

Reflex is primarily a save against enemy attempts to place a character under an effect. It can also be used as a save when the character experiences sudden unexpected (and usually very negative) situations.

For example, if your character has to climb a rope to cross a deep chasm, rolling very low on an Acrobatics check could result in your character falling to their death. Rather than having such high stakes rely upon a single roll, the GM may allow the falling character a Reflex save to try to catch the rope again.

In combat, retreat may sometimes be the most viable strategy. A Disengage Action therefore represents an attempt to leave an enemy's melee range, but as you will see in the Attack of Opportunity section, this may give the enemy a potential opportunity to attack even though it is not their turn. The disengage is the fleeing character's attempt to avoid this attack, by spending an Action of their own to contest the enemy's atk with a Reflex ability roll.

Stealth

Sneaking represents an attempt to prevent an enemy from knowing where you are or what you are doing. While, mathematically, this is represented by a simple ability check, the actual chance of success is much more complicated:

At minimum, you will likely have to break line-of-sight, and reduce the noises you make. You'll have to carefully examine the battlemap and decide where you want to position yourself, and then move as little as possible.

Unfortunately, your GM will most likely not tell you if your attempt at stealth is successful or not until the consequences are already self-evident...

In combat, if you manage to stay hidden, the first attack you make from a successful stealth attempt counts as a Sneak Attack, which means the dmg roll for the atk will be multiplied by 2.

Successful Sneak Attack = dmg *2

Thievery

Thievery is the ability that represents the subtler, and often not entirely legal, arts requiring delicate manual dexterity. Lockpicking, trap-disarming, pickpocketing, etc, all fall into this category.

As a combat action, pickpocketing represents an attempt to manipulate your enemy's inventory, for example, grabbing a key from their belt, or switching one of their healing potions for a poison flask from your own inventory. Obviously, if the enemy has already spotted you, the DC for the attempt will be higher, but distractions in the form of Grappling or Performance rolls can sometimes help you achieve success.

For obvious reasons, pickpocketing can only be performed if you are in melee range of your target.

Crafting

Crafting is one of those abilities that are often overlooked, as you will rarely be asked to make a Crafting roll. Nevertheless, characters trained in a craft can perform remarkable feats with it, and usually have a much easier time acquiring gold than those who are not.

The Crafting modifier becomes important when you are spending some down-time to collect ingredients:

# of ingredients collected per hour = 1d4 + Crafting Proficiency

For every point of Proficiency bonus your character has, they can memorize a recipe to craft an item corresponding to that profession. The Armory contains some ideas for items, but you can also create custom items for yourself. You and your GM will then have to figure out the ingredient cost for your custom recipes before you can actually create them in the game.

For instance, if your chosen craft is Alchemy, you could use one of your recipe memory slots to memorize how to make a healing potion. While it is entirely possible to purchase a recipe from a merchant, or even find it while you are out adventuring, those recipes that are not memorized can only be created as long as you have the recipe in your inventory, meaning it can be stolen from you, or the page might get destroyed while you cross a river... Memorized recipes cannot be lost.

Note that the INT modifier that usually applies to an ability does not count towards your recipe limit, but it does get factored in when you are collecting ingredients or calculating hourly wage, as described below.

Recipe Limit = Crafting Proficiency (without INT modifier)

Another way to use the Crafting ability is to calculate a better wage for skilled workers compared to those without a learned craft (keep in mind that you can only earn this better wage if there is demand for it - for example, you can only get a blacksmith's pay if you are actually working as a blacksmith):

In-game Hourly wage = Crafting Proficiency (minimum 2 gold per hour)

History

History checks can be rolled when you are trying to remember something specific to your past. This can include knowledge that your character would have based on their backstory - even if the player that controls the character doesn't have that knowledge, or it can mean small bits of information that your GM may have given you before, but you just need a little reminder.

In combat, a History check is essential when special enemy types appear on the battlefield. Certain enemies will either be immune to certain damage types, or may even be healed or strengthened by them instead. Think of a fire elemental, for example. Not only does it not care about you waving a torch in its face, but stoking its flames would actually make it stronger. The History check is therefore there to try to remember any special information about what the enemy may be vulnerable to, or resistant against.

Investigation

Investigation comes into play when you are trying to figure out brand new information. Where a History check refers to specific information you have learned before, Investigation can help you figure out something you never knew before.

Investigation is also useful when you are searching for something, such as a trap, or a clue about something. In this context, it overlaps slightly with Perception, but Investigation is more of a hands- on search, looking under that pile of leaves or questioning suspects, while Perception is simply observation without interference.

In combat, it can also be useful to take a second to pick your target's weak spot, especially if your enemy has not seen you yet. It essentially sacrifices one action in order to make the next attack stronger:

  • less than 0 => no bonus
  • 0 to 4 => +1 atk and +2 dmg
  • 5 to 9 => +2 atk and +4 dmg
  • 10 to 14 => +3 atk and +6 dmg
  • 15 to 19 => +4 atk and +8 dmg
  • 20 to 24 => +5 atk and +10 dmg
  • 25 to 29 => +6 atk and +12 dmg

Intuition

If you're stuck and not really sure what to do, you can also use an Intuition check to essentaily ask your GM for some hints. Think of it as a form of 'sixth sense'. A History check can only give you information that your PC would reasonably know based on their backstory and previous encounters, and an Investigation can lead to logically formed conclusion, but a good Intuition check might allow you to infer brand new information right in the heat of the moment.

Despite this vague description, Intuition comes up rather often, as it bridges the gap that may occur between GM and players based on their own thinking styles. If your GM is giving you information and you just can't seem to put the pieces of the puzzle together, it might be worth asking for an Intuition check as well.

Perception

This is a quick physical check of your surroundings. If you suspect that there might be enemies in hiding, waiting to ambush you, or if you think an area might be trapped, or even just if you really enjoy a scene your GM is describing, it may be a good idea to spend an Action just making sure you have a good lay of the land before interacting.

As for all abilities in HammRPG, if you do not make a specific roll, your Passive ability is 0 + your modifiers. In other words, even if you get the lowest possible roll on your ability check, you will get more information than simply waiting for the GM to tell you what's happening. Some GMs will wait for you to ask them for a Perception check, rather than potentially giving you a hint that there might be something hidden by asking you for it.

Survival

Survival represents your character's navigational abilities, especially in nature, as well as their ability to communicate when they do not speak the same language as their counterpart, which often comes into play when trying to communicate with animals. Survival is also a common check when you are affected by effects such as Blinded or Deafened, or when you are in the middle of a dust-cloud or smoke-screen.

Willpower

Willpower is a common save against enemy attempts to use magic against a character. If your character is ever captured, you can also use a Willpower roll to see how well they are resisting hostile attempts to interrogate your PC, etc.

Inspiration

If you have a very critical roll coming up, you can psych yourself up for it using your own internal monologue as Inspiration (Note that this costs an Action in combat, unless you are spending PP):

  • less than 0 => no bonus
  • 0 to 9 => +1 next ability roll
  • 10 to 19 => +2 next ability roll
  • 20 to 29 => +3 next ability roll

Leadership

Leadership functions as a measure of your disciplice and your ability to inspire others. As such, it is the default save against enemy attempts to Intimidate you.

In the same spirit as the Inspiration Action above, you can also spend an Action to help one of your allies. The bonuses for helping your friends are higher, though, especially if you roll well:

  • less than 0 => no bonus
  • 0 to 4 => +1
  • 5 to 9 => +2
  • 10 to 14 => +3
  • 15 to 19 => +4
  • 20 to 24 => +5
  • 25 to 29 => +6

When you spend PP to help your allies instead of yourself, you will also be able to use the Leadership roll instead of the Inspiration roll, which makes helping others more effective than helping yourself, specifically in order to encourage teamwork.

Performance

Performance refers to your ability to handle the spotlight. Whether performing in a tavern or running a distraction while your allies steal something or sneak up on a critical enemy, Performance has a huge variety of applications. The GM will determine the DC of the roll based on the specific context, including your role-play for the distraction and/or any details you describe.

Persuasion

Persuasion is the art of word-craft, which means it will most commonly come into play when you role-play conversations and similar situations. Some spells, particularly charms and such, can also utilize the Persuasion ability for their applicable contests.

If you or your GM do not enjoy the haggling experience, you can also use Persuasion as an easy way to calculate discounts when buying or selling items. The formula for this is given in the Charisma section of the Attributes page; however, using Persuasion rather than the base CHA modifier allows you to take advantage of Persuasion proficiency. Which of these two methods you end up using should be discussed during the Session Zero for your game.
