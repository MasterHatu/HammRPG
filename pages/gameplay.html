The HammRPG Concept

HammRPG is a tabletop roleplaying game (TTRPG) that allows players to collectively create and narrate a story together. One player takes on the role of the Game Master (GM), who drives the story forward and plays all the non-player characters (NPCs), while the other players create their own player characters (PCs) and work together with the GM to tell a unique and exciting story.

HammRPG is a collaborative storytelling game. This means that the GM and players all work together to create a story through cooperation and teamwork. No one player is more important than the others and everyone has a say in how the story unfolds by roleplaying their PC's choices, actions, and conversations. The more you immerse yourself into your character and try to think and act from their perspective, the more fun everyone will have. That being said, keep in mind that the player behind the character is always more important than the character or the even the story itself!

The main concept that sets HammRPG apart from other TTRPGs is its focus on customizability. Where some games may have you choose certain abilities from a pre-made list of possible features, HammRPG allows each player to create their own entirely customized abilities from scratch. Your character can be as synergized or ramshackled as you like, providing for a virtually infinitely complex combination of character features and roleplay opportunities. In order ensure all players have the same opportunity to shine, we recommend using the Ability Guide to create your custom abilities. This will enable you to plan how your PC will develop over time, helps with balancing, and might also provide some ideas for abilities you may not have thought of.

The below rules section is designed to help players create a fun and engaging storytelling experience. It provides a framework for resolving conflicts, handling challenges, and advancing the story in a way that is hopefully fair to everyone at the table. However, it's important to remember that the rules are not meant to be restrictive - the group should always feel free to adapt the rules as needed to best serve their specific story.

Character Level

Mathematically, the main measurement of advancement and power of a character is the character level (Lv).

The lowest possible Lv is Lv1, and while theoretically there is no upper limit to Lv, the higher you go the more complicated the game becomes. As the number of abilities continues to increase for both PCs and NPCs, it becomes increasingly difficult to keep track of everything, and so, for practicality, we typically scale NPCs up to approximately Lv20, as shown below (Note that this is only a rough guide, and many exceptions may exist at the discretion of the GM.):

  • Lv1 to Lv3 - Slaves, prisoners, and other characters that have been deliberately weakened
  • Lv4 to Lv6 - Average citizens, grunt soldiers, or common animals
  • Lv7 to Lv8 - Common soldiers, city guards, predator animals
  • Lv9 to Lv10 - Mercenaries, experienced soldiers, stronger predators
  • Lv11 to Lv13 - Professional soldiers, military captains, some weak monsters
  • Lv14 to Lv17 - Generals, guild leaders, more monsters
  • Lv17 to Lv20 - Heroes, famous warriors, stronger monsters
  • Lv20 and up - Legendary figures, epic monsters, etc.

Every character starts with 60 Attribute Points (AP) and 3 Background Points (BP). Additionally, the character also gains 1 additional AP, as well as 3 Skill Points (SP) per Lv. Some GMs further allow their players to earn additional BP at certain Lvs, such as 3 base, +1 at Lv 5, +2 at Lv 10, +1 at Lv 15, and +3 at Lv20; however, this is optional at the GM's discretion, and will be discussed during the Session Zero for your specific game. The different types of points will be discussed in detail further below.

Levelling Up

One of the main motivations for many players is the opportunity to advance and develop their character. Of course, there any many ways to show character growth through roleplay, but the primary way to represent character growth is by increasing your PC's level, by collecting Experience Points (XP).

In order to level up, a PC needs to gain the same number of XP as the Lv they are trying to reach. In other words, if a PC is Lv4, their next Lv would be Lv5. Therefore, the PC needs to collect 5 XP.

For every significant encounter, all surviving PCs gain 1 XP. The definitiion of 'significant' in this context is deliberately left vague, as it can vary between groups, as well as based on the Lv of the PCs involved. For instance, going to a shop to buy a new sword is probably not significant regardless of PC Lv, but fighting off a group of three zombies is significant for a Lv 1 or 2 PC, while it is quite insignificant for a Lv 15 PC, or even a group of three Lv 5 PCs. As such, it is usually up the GM to determine if an encounter is significant enough to warrant XP.

Typically, this method is used to collect separate XP per individual PC, but some groups prefer to pool their XP (both needed and collected), so that they can advance and grow together. This is up to the group to determine during their Session Zero before the actual game starts

Attributes

HammRPG uses six common attributes to define a character's general affinity with certain abilities: Constitution (CON), Strength (STR), Dexterity (DEX), Intelligence (INT), Wisdom (WIS) and Charisma (CHA).

Attributes are used in two different ways:

  1. The attribute value itself.
  2. An ability modifier based on that attribute.

For all attributes, HammRPG uses the full name (e.g.: Constitution) when referring to the attribute itself, while we use use the abbreviation (e.g.: CON) when referring to the modifier.

Attribute Point Distribution

There are various ways to allocate Attribute Points (AP) in your specific game. Which system you use should be discussed during the Session Zero before you actually start playing.

The main method of attribute point distribution endorsed by HammRPG is sometimes called Point-Buy: All characters start with 60 Attribute Points (AP), and gain 1 additional AP per Lv. These points can be allocated to any of the attributes entirely at the player's preference. This is the default method, as it allows complete player freedon in their character creation, and results in characters that should be at least reasonably similar in power compared to each other. Of course, in a game as customizable as HammRPG, complete balance is nearly impossible, as one set of abilities might synergize better than another.

Another popular method is Stat-Rolling: Each player rolls three six-sided dice (3d6) six times. The results of the rolls are either appended to the attributes in order, or at the player's preference, per group discussion. This method introduces an element of randomness, which can result in wildly different characters strength levels. While experienced players may relish the challenge of adjusting their play-style according to what character they rolled, newer players may find it more difficult, especially if they ended up with a below average character. Additionally, most enemies in the GM guide are balanced around the idea that characters will have a base of 60 AP.

Note that AP (as well as most other types of points) can only be changed when the character is created, or during level-up events, which usually require a full rest.

Attribute Modifiers

Modifiers are calculated based on the attribute most relevant to the roll. An attribute score of 10 is considered average, resulting in no modifiers to rolls based on that attribute. If a character has an attribute score lower than 10, they will have a negative modifier to those rolls, while an attribute higher than 10 will grant a bonus to those rolls.

The formula for calculating your modifier is:

modifier = ( attribute - 10 ) / 2

Note: Only integer values are valid for modifier calculations. A result of less than 1 is always rounded down to the nearest full number.

E.g. if a character has an attribute value of 11, their modifier is (11-10)/2=0.5 => 0.

Constitution

Constitution (CON) is a measure of a character's toughness, and their ability to withstand damage. The most direct way to represent this is with the number of Health Points (HP) the character has:

maxHP = Constitution * Lv

Based on this formula, you can see that maxHP increases with higher values of Constitution, as well as the character level itself. As such, a character with high Constitution will continue to gain larger amounts of maxHP per level-up than one with low Constitution.

Constitution also affects Physical Resistance (PR), which further helps your character withstand damage. The PR modifier works the same as the regular CON modifier, except that it does not have negative values, even if your Constitutionis less than 10.

If CON < 0:

PR = 0

If CON >= 0:

PR = CON

Please refer to the damage calculation guide below for further details on PR.

The Endurance and Resistance abilities are also based on Constitution.

Strength

Strength (STR) is a measure of the physical force that a character can exert. One of its largest benefits is its effect on Melee Damage (MD), which will cause your melee attacks to hit harder. Just as PR does not have negative values, MD also does not decrease a damage roll's (dmg) result.

If STR < 0:

MD = 0

If STR >= 0:

MD = STR

Please refer to the damage calculation guide below for further details on MD.

Strength also affects the Athletics, Grappling and Intimidation abilities.

Dexterity

Dexterity (DEX) is a measure of agility and flexibility. A character with a high Dexterity attribute is better able to perform jumps and other complicated maneuvers, avoid or disable traps, and may even be able to dodge incoming attacks entirely.

Dodge Value (DV), which allows characters to completely avoid attacks, is calculated similar to other modifiers, however, it scales only half as rapidly:

DV = ( DEX - 10 ) / 4

It is important to note that DV does not make a character invulnerable: Area damage, such as an exploding fireball, a cloud of poison, a collapsing wall, or a shockwave from an artillery shell, applies directly, without any chance to dodge. In such cases, the GM may call for a Reflex or Resistance save to reduce the damage, but DV itself will not come into play.

Dexterity also affects the Acrobatics, Reflex, Stealth and Thievery abilities.

Intelligence

Intelligence (INT) represents a character's ability to use their mind through their Mana Points (MP):

maxMP = Intelligence * Lv

As you can see, the formula is the same as the formula for maxHP, except instead of using Constitution as a base, MP are based on INT. As MP are needed to use active abilities, this means that characters with a high Intelligence attribute will be able to use their active abilities more often than those with low Intelligence.

Intelligence also comes into play as a form of roleplay immersion. For instance, an NPC with a low Intelligence attribute is less likely to use advanced combat tactics than one with high Intelligence. Many players also enjoy roleplaying these details, as it creates a fun atmosphere for everyone.

The abilities based on Intelligence focus more on roleplaying than on pure combat stats, through Crafting, History, Investigation, and Mindfulness. However, a smart player can still use this attribute to create a powerful character.

Wisdom

Wisdom (WIS) represents a character's attunement with the world around them, including magic. Most magic based abilities use the WIS modifier to enhance magical damage rolls, and a high Wisdom attribute also improves magical reisitance (MR) to defend against enemy spells. The formula is very similar to that for PR:

If WIS < 0:

MR = 0

If WIS >= 0:

MR = WIS

Therefore, a character with a Wisdom attribute of 12, would automatically have an MR value of +1. Please refer to the damage calculation guide below for further details on MR.

Wisdom further affects the ability rolls for Intuition, Perception, Survival and Willpower.

Charisma

Charisma (CHA) is the attribute least applicable to combat, but that does not make it any weaker than the other attributes. A character with high Charisma benefits from significant advantages during roleplay elements of a game, including the potential to avoid combat altogether. Meanwhile, a character with a particularly low Charisma score may find themselves unfortunately vulnerable to spells that affect the mind, which range from card tricks and other small illusions to game-changing charms or mind-controls. Nevertheless, Charisma remains one of the subtler attributes.

Groups who do not enjoy the haggling experience in their games can also use the CHA modifier to quickly calculate discounts when their characters visit merchants to purchase items:

When Purchasing:

Purchase Price
=
Armory Price * ( 1.5 - ( CHA / 50 ) ) * Local Supply Modifier

When Selling:

Selling Price
=
Armory Price * ( 0.5 + ( CHA / 50 ) ) * Local Supply Modifier

The Inspiration, Leadership, Performance, and Persuasion abilities are based on Charisma.

Background Points

Background Points (BP) are what makes your character unique. These points can be spent to acquire powerful abilities that will define your PC and their playstyle throughout the entire game.

While not strictly required, HammRPG recommends writing a backstory for your PC first, and only then creating abilities that make sense within that backstory. Characters whose abilities are strongly intertwined with their backstories tend to be more interesting from a role-play perspective, which improves immersion for yourself as well as everyone else at the table.

Please refer to the Character Creation guide as well as the Ability Creation guide, and talk to your GM to create your own custom abilities.

Skill Points

Skill Points (SP) are very similar to BP, except that they are less powerful. For instance, a single BP could grant your character a +5 bonus for a given ability, while a SP would only grant a +1.

The biggest advantage of SP over BP is that you get a lot more of them, which allows you to diversify your PC much more. Additionally, SP are designed to be a more fluid representation of your PC's current abilities, rather than a fixed picture of their background. As a result, most GMs will make it much easier to change SP abilities than BP abilities (see PP section below).

The two most common types of abilities are proficiencies, which provide a bonus to ability or attack rolls (atk), and active abilities, which use MP to achieve much stronger bonuses to certain individual Actions alone.

Please refer to the Character Creation guide as well as the Ability Creation guide, and talk to your GM to create your own custom abilities.

Encounters

While outside of combat situations, your character is able to do essentially anything you can think of. When an encounter starts, the GM will typically describe the situation first, then the players have an opportunity to interact with that situation. Depending on what the players choose to do, the GM will then ask for appropriate ability rolls to determine how well your character actually performs what you described, or respond from the perspective of the NPCs the players interacted with, etc. The result of the roll, or the result of the role-play then leads to certain consequences as described by the GM. This back-and-forth between describing, rolling and interacting is the essence of HammRPG.

As such, while not in combat, actions and abilities are relatively fluid and intuitive, rather than closely defined. Nevertheless, you can find a general description of each of the Abilities below.

However, if a situation escalates into combat, time becomes a much more significant factor. This is where HammRPG uses a turn-based combat system to slow down time in order to allow players (and the GM) to think about their next move, describe their attacks or other Actions in epic detail, and still banter between each other without the stress of real-time combat.

Dice Rolling

Many of the actions described below are resolved by rolling dice. In such cases, HammRPG uses the XdX notation style:

# of Dice + "d" + Type of Dice

For example, one twenty-sided die is written as 1d20, three twelve-sided dice would be 3d12, etc.

Normally, a roll consists of a single instance of rolling the appropriate dice, and then adding the appropriate modifiers to the roll. However, in some instances a roll may be rolled with Disadvantage, or Advantage, which means that we:

If Disadvantage

roll twice and take the lower number

If Advantage

roll twice and take the higher number

The instances of disadvantage or advantage stack, which means that if there are multiple sources of advantage or disadvantage, the number of total dice rolled also increases. Likewise, advantage and disadvantage cancel each other out.

For example, let's say our PC has a situation where they need to make a roll with 4 sources of Advantage and 2 sources of Disadvantage: Two of the advantage-disadvantage pair cancel each other out, and we are left with 2 instances of Advantage. As a result, we roll 3 dice (1 base, plus 1 for each instance of advantage), and then take the highest resulting number.

Any applicable modifiers are only applied after the roll itself, including advantage-disadvantage considerations, have been completed.

Abilities

All characters have acccess to the same set of basic Abilities, which are influenced by the most appropriate Attribute:

Endurance

Endurance is a character's cardio. In essence, how long can an effort be sustained before you run out of steam. While there is no specific combat-Action based on Endurance, your GM can ask you for an Endurance roll if you perform repeated Sprinting attempts or other strenuous tasks.

Endurance can also be used to help calculate travel times for long distance trips, etc.

Resistance

Resistance is one of the less-used abilities. Generally, it applies as a save against hostile attempts to affect your character, such as Grappling attempts or attacks that attempt to stun your character.

Some GMs also like to use the Resistance ability to reduce the potency of certain effects, such as poisons, but this is up to the GM and should be discussed during your group's Session Zero.

Athletics

Athletics is a measure of the direct physical force a character's body can muster on short notice. Trying to push a heavy object, lift a rock, or compete in an arm-wrestling match are all typical examples that would warrant an Athletics roll.

In combat, the most common Athletics check you will see is the Sprint Action:

A Sprint Action is an attempt to gain additional Movement so that you can move across the battlefield more quickly, or run away from an overpowering enemy. If you spend a combat-Action to try to sprint:

  • less than 0 => no bonus Movement (the Action is still spent, though!)
  • 0 to 9 => +1 m of Movement
  • 10 to 19 => +2 m of Movement
  • 20 to 29 => +3 m of Movement
  • etc.

Grappling

Grappling is an attempt to hold an enemy down, preventing them from moving and using Actions freely. This can be done to protect your allies, prevent an enemy from running away, or simply to stop a spellcaster from casting. Grappling is almost exclusively a combat action, which means if you attempt it in a non-combat encounter, it will usually turn into a combat encounter.

First, the initiating grappler rolls their atk roll, which is a Grappling ability roll with Unarmed Proficiency. The resulting number is then compared to the target's DV to see if the target simply dodges the attack altogether. Finally, if the target does not dodge, the defender also rolls a Grappling roll to determine who gains control in the struggle. The initial Grappling attempt does not deal any damage.

If the attacker gains control, the Grapple is maintained, and the attacker continues their turn. If the defender gains control, they may choose to immediately let go of the Grapple, or maintain it as is. Regardless of who gains control, both contestants gain the Grappled effect as long as the Grapple is maintained.

While grappled, the contestants can only perform the following Actions (rolling with disadvantage unless they are in control of the contest):

  • Gain Control: The character performing this Action simply re-rolls their Grappling attempt. The DC for this is calculated according to the normal DC formula, based on the opponent's most recent Grappling roll. This Action can also be used to improve already existing control of a Grappling contest; however, it does not deal any damage.
  • Shove Action: The initiator rolls Athletics, while the defender rolls Resistance. The winner may then dictate up to 1 m of movement for each 5 points of lead in the contest. For example, if the contest results are 5 vs 18, the character that rolled the 18 can move both characters by up to 2 m each ((18 - 5) / 5 = 2). Such Movment is only possible if the winner actually has the necessary Movement available, but the looser's Movement is ignored within this context.
  • Unarmed Attack
  • Intimidation Action
  • Pickpocket Action
  • Knowledge Check
  • Intuition Check
  • Perception Check
  • Inspiration Action
  • Distraction Action

Intimidation

An Intimidation Action is an attempt to frighten the enemy, which is represented by the Feared effect when in combat. It functions similar to the Grappling contest, except that the initiator rolls Intimidation, while the target rolls Leadership as a measure of their level of discipline.

Intimidation is usually specific to the one target, but can in some cases also apply to a group of targets. In such situations, the GM will ususally increase the DC based on how many targets are around. In other words, the more allies a target has around them, the higher their chance of resisting an Intimidation attempt.

Acrobatics

Acrobatics represents a character's general agility.

In many instances, a character may need to perform a Jump Action, for example to cross a chasm, to avoid triggering a pressure plate trap, etc.:

  • less than 0 => the jump critically fails, which may mean the PC falls into the chasm, triggers the trap, etc. without the opportunity to try to save the situation
  • 0 to 4 => the jump fails, but the PC may roll a Reflex save to stop themselves. The DC for the save is 20 - the existing Acrobatics roll
  • 5 to 9 => the jump clears up to 1 m
  • 10 to 14 => the jump clears up to 2 m
  • 15 to 19 => the jump clears up to 3 m
  • 20 to 24 => the jump clears up to 4 m
  • 25 to 29 => the jump clears up to 5 m
  • etc.

Note that the Jump distance does count towards your Movement when you are in combat. In other words, if your PC has only 3 tiles of Movement left, they can only jump up to 3 tiles, even if they roll a wicked Acrobatics check (let's say a 42 or something like that).

Disengage Action (Reflex)

In many situations, retreat may be the most viable strategy. Disengaging represents an attempt to leave an enemy's melee range, but as you will see in the Attack of Opportunity section, this gives them a potential opportunity to attack you even though it is not their turn. A disengage is your attempt to avoid this attack, by spending an Action of your own to contest the enemy's atk with your Reflex ability, giving you a second chance to get away from the enemy.

Sneak Action (Stealth)

Sneaking represents an attempt to prevent an enemy from knowing where you are or what you are doing. While, mathematically, this is represented by a simple ability check, the actual chance of success is much more complicated:

At minimum, you will likely have to break line-of-sight, and reduce the noises you make. You'll have to carefully examine the battlemap and decide where you want to position yourself, and then move as little as possible.

Unfortunately, your GM will most likely not tell you if your attempt at stealth is successful or not until the consequences are already self-evident...

If you manage to stay hidden, the first attack you make from stealth counts as a Sneak Attack, which means the dmg roll for the atk will be multiplied by 2.

Successful Sneak Attack = dmg *2

Pickpocket Action (Thievery)

As a combat action, pickpocketing represents an attempt to manipulate your enemy's inventory. This can be straightforward as grabbing a key from their belt, or as subtle as switching one of their healing potions for a poison flask from your own inventory. Obviously, if the enemy has already spotted you, the DC for the attempt will be higher, but distractions in the form of Grappling or Performance rolls can sometimes help you achieve success.

For obvious reasons, this action can only be performed if you are in melee range of your target.

Knowledge Check (History)

A History check is essential when special enemy types appear on the battlefield. Certain enemies will either be immune to certain damage types, or may even be healed or strengthened by them instead. Think of a fire elemental, for example. Not only does it not care about you waving a torch in its face, but stoking its flames would actually make it stronger.

The History check is therefore there to try to find out what the enemy may be vulnerable to, or resistant against.

Targeting Action (Investigation)

Taking a second to pick your target's weak spot is useful, especially if your enemy has not seen you yet. It essentially sacrifices one action in order to make the next attack stronger:

  • less than 0 => no bonus
  • 0 to 4 => +1 atk and +2 dmg
  • 5 to 9 => +2 atk and +4 dmg
  • 10 to 14 => +3 atk and +6 dmg
  • 15 to 19 => +4 atk and +8 dmg
  • 20 to 24 => +5 atk and +10 dmg
  • 25 to 29 => +6 atk and +12 dmg

Intuition Check

If you're stuck and not really sure what to do, you can also use an Intuition check to essentaily ask your GM for some hints. A Knowledge check can only give you information that your PC would reasonably know based on their backstory and previous encounters, but a good Intuition check might allow you to infer brand new conclusions.

Perception Check

This is a quick physical check of your surroundings. If you suspect that there might be enemies in hiding, waiting to ambush you, or if you think an area might be trapped, it may be a good idea to spend an Action just making sure you have a good lay of the land before charging in.

As for all abilities, if you do not make a specific roll, your Passive Perception is 0 + your modifiers. In other words, even if you get the lowest possible roll on your ability check, you will get more information than simply waiting for the GM to tell you what's happening. Some GMs will wait for you to ask them for a Perception check, rather than potentially giving you a hint that there might be something hidden by asking you for it.

Inspiration Action

If you have a very critical roll coming up, you can spnd an Action to psych yourself up for it. This is very similar to the Targeting Action for atk rolls, but for ability checks. Note that the bonuses are reduced:

  • less than 0 => no bonus
  • 0 to 9 => +1 next ability roll
  • 10 to 19 => +2 next ability roll
  • 20 to 29 => +3 next ability roll

You will also roll Inspiration every time you spend a Party Point on yourself, although in that case it does not cost you an Action.

Help Others Action (Leadership)

In the same spirit as the Inspiration Action, you can also spend an Action to help one of your allies. These bonuses are higher, though, especially if you roll well:

  • less than 0 => no bonus
  • 0 to 4 => +1
  • 5 to 9 => +2
  • 10 to 14 => +3
  • 15 to 19 => +4
  • 20 to 24 => +5
  • 25 to 29 => +6

When you spend Party Points on your allies instead of yourself, you will also be able to use the Leadership roll instead of the Inspiration roll.

Distraction Action (Performance)

This is essentially the opposite of a Sneak Action, and can be used to help take the attention away from your allies so that they can perform a critical task more safely, or to lure enemies into a trap. The GM will determine the DC of the roll based on the specific context, including your role-play for the distraction.

Combat

The following sections are specific to combat encounters; however, this is not an exhaustive list. In many cases, your GM will have to make a situation-specific ruling, which then supercedes the rules described here.

Initiative

In order to determine who gets to go first during turn based combat, HammRPG uses Initiative rolls, which is essentially a simple Reflex ability roll.

Initiative = Reflex roll = 1d20 + Reflex modifier

Some groups prefer to utilize individual initiatives, while others prefer to allow all PCs to play their turn at the same time, and then play all the NPC's turn at the same time. Group initiative makes cooperation easier for groups who prefer it, and may speed up combat in online text-only campaigns, while individual initiative tends to make for cleaner combat if players like to lean heavily into roleplay, as well as for high level groups. This is a matter of preference and will be discussed during the Session Zero before the campaign starts.

Movement

At Lv1, each player has 5 m of Movement per turn.

Unless otherwise specified, HammRPG considers 1 tile on a given battlemap to be 1 meter (m) by 1 m in size, for simplicity. However, additional Movement can be purchased with BP or SP, as outlined in the Character Creation Guide and the Ability Creation Guide

Actions

An Action is a basic activity that a player performs. Swinging a sword at an enemy is one of the simplest examples of this, but there are several basic Actions that every character can perform even without purchasing these Actions with BP or SP. Please refer to the Abilities List for specific descriptions on how your Abilities can be used in a combat encounter.

By default, each character has 1 Action, and can purchase more with SP or BP. Some GMs also grant additional Actions at certain levels, just to increase the speed of combat encounters and allow players to feel more powerful.

Reactions

A Reaction is very similar to an Action, except that it is something you can do when it is not your turn. The only default Reaction is the Attack of Opportunity.

By default, each character has 1 Reaction, and can purchase more with SP or BP.

Melee Attack Action

The attack Action is the most basic Action in combat encounters. You can either just use your hands or feet or you can use an equipped weapon. Your specific selection of possible attack Actions will depend on the skills and items your PC has available. All melee attack Actions are rolled with a 1d20, plus any bonuses from your character's proficiencies, or the weapon's own modifiers, where the final result represents the attack roll (atk).

atk = 1d20 + Proficiencies + Weapon Modifiers

The result of atk is then compared to the target's DV; If atk is equal to or higher than the DV, the attack is successful, and you move on to the damage calculations, which will be explained in detail further below, or apply the appropriate effects. The type and number of dice to use for the dmg roll depends on what attack Action you performed.

For instance, an unarmed attack uses the unarmed proficiency for atk and a 1d4 damage die. If this is a melee attack, it also benefits from the MD modifier. Similarly, a Steel Shortsword uses sword proficiency, but it also provides a bonus of +1 for atk due to its own design, as well as a 1d6+MD for dmg.

The Armory contains a list of commonly available items with their roll modifiers and other important stats.

Melee Attack Failure:

There are no special considerations, as the attack likely just swings through the air. Some GMs like to apply immersive consequences on a nat1 (hit yourself, hit a nearby ally, drop your weapon, etc.), but this is optional and should be discussed during the Session Zero for your game.

Damage Calculation

If an attack successfully hits the target, the next step is to determine how hard the hit lands. The Damage Roll (dmg) dice are usually determined by the specific weapons being used. A default unarmed attack uses 1d4 dice, while a particularly good weapon may have 2d12 or even larger dice. Additionally, some weapons provide intrinsic modifiers to the damage dice as well, as detailed in either the Armory or the item's specific stats.

All melee attacks also benefit from the MD modifier, which is based on the character's Strength, as well as MD proficiency:

Melee Attack:

dmg = XdX + Item Modifiers + MD

Ranged Attack

dmg = XdX + Item Modifiers

However, different people experience pain differently, and an unarmored character will suffer much more than one in heavy plate armor. This concept is represented by the character's PR and MR stats. As such, we need to determine if the attack was physical or magical in nature, and the subtract the appropriate modifer from the dmg:

Physical dmg:

actual HP loss = dmg - PR

Magical dmg

actual HP loss = dmg - MR

Note that these modifiers can never reduce your actual HP loss below 1. If an attack hits you, but the dmg is lower than your PR/MR, you will always suffer at least 1 point of HP loss.

Ranged Attack Action

If you cammot reach your enemy, you can still try to attack them by throwing objects (rocks, bottles, alchemical flasks, chairs, etc.), or using ranged weapons such as bows, crossbows, guns, etc. The damage calculation for ranged weapons is idential to the Damage Calculation for melee weapons, except that ranged weapons do not by default benefit from the MD modifier.

One important thing to keep in mind with virtually all ranged weapons, is ammunition; you can only shoot an arrow if you actually have an arrow to shoot. Some improvised items, such as rocks, might be available in your surroundings, or you can carry such ammunition with you in your inventory. It is up to you, the player, to ensure that you keep track of ammunition for your PC.

Additionally, characters can use Cover to help avoid ranged attacks

Ranged Weapon Attacks

Most specialized ranged attack tools have their own unique effective ranges. For example, when using a shortbow from the Armory, we can see that the standard range is 40 m, while the maximum range is up to 400 m. This means that, when attacking a target that is within 40 m of our character, we simply roll atk with proficiencies. However, if the target is in the long range, we must roll atk with disadvantage, which scales based on the standard range.

In our example above, this means that for every 40 m, we need to consider an additional instance of disadvantage. Up to 40 m we roll a single die (no disadvantage), between 40 m and 80 m we roll two dice and take the lower result (disadvantage), between 80 m and 120 m we roll three dice and take the lowest result (double disadvantage), then between 120 m and 160 m we roll 4 dice and take the lowest result (triple disadvantage), etc.

The maximum range indicates the absoulte maximum at which a shot can be attempted, although in many instances, it is probably not practical to actually try to make a shot at that range due to the scaling disadvantage consideration.

Standard Range

Single atk roll + proficiency modifiers

Long Range

+1 instance of disadvantage per multiplier of the standard range number

Ranged Throwing Attacks

Throwing, on the other hand, is more a feature of the skill of the thrower, as there is no tool being used. As such, throwing is handled through a combined atk/ability roll - in other words, an atk roll that also benefits from the ability modifier:

When throwing for impact damage:
(e.g.: rock or spear throw)

Athletics roll, where DC = range

When throwing for targeting precision:
(e.g.: poison flask, distracting noise)

Acrobatics roll, where DC = range

As such throwing does not have an inherent maximum range, but rather your character's skills determine how far they can realistically throw. A beneficial side effect of this method is that both your Throwing proficiency and your Athletics or Acrobatics proficiency applies, which makes throwing a potentially very powerful combat method at early levels.

Regardless of the range of a throw though, the target's DV still applies, and a throwing attack will miss if it is lower than the target's DV, even if it is higher than the DC needed for the throw.

Ranged Failure Handling

When a melee attack fails, it most likely simply swings through the air, which we can easily ignore. However, once a ranged projectile has been fired or thrown, it might still hit something else, even if it misses its intended target. Particularly, projectiles that deal area damage, such as flasks of poison that shatter on impact and release a poison cloud, or exploding artillery shells, are important in this context.

We can therefore have to consider targeting accuracy when a ranged attack fails. If the attack misses:

For ranged weapon attacks:

Offset = (range to target / default range ) * ( DV - atk )

For ranged throw attacks:

Offset = DC - atk

You can then roll a 1d8 to determine in which direction the projectlie missed. A 1 would mean the projectile lands north of the target, 2 northeast, 3 east, etc.

Note that this system is rather complicated, and it is entirely reasonable to simply have the GM make an on the spot ruling on where the projectile landed based on how bad the atk roll was. For non-area damage or effect causing ranged attacks, a miss can simply be a miss without figuring out where else the projectile goes.

Cover

Another optional system for ranged weapons is the effect blocking projectiles by utilizing cover.

Different blocking objects provide different levels of protection:

  • Light Cover (heavy foliage, picket fence, light post, etc.): +2 DV
  • Medium Cover (wooden wall, storage crates, etc.): +5 DV
  • Heavy Cover (stone wall, metal plate objects, etc.): +10 to DV
  • Complete Cover: Ranged attacks are not possible

Note that peeking out from cover, for example in order to make a ranged attack yourself, reduces the effect of whatever cover you are using by 50%. Additionally, it is not possible to peek out from complete cover.
